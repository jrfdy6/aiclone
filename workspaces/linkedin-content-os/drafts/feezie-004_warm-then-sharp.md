## First-pass draft

Look, I used to confuse being the loudest with being the clearest. I'd walk into an enrollment war room, clock the holes in the plan, and start issuing fixes before anybody finished their sentence. Decisions stuck because people were afraid to push back, not because they believed in the move.

Warm then sharp changed that. These days I let the team talk first, I repeat the part I agree with, and I ask one question that forces us to get specific. Only after that do I draw the line on what cannot slip. When we were rebuilding Fusion's referral pipeline, that shift meant the recruiters heard that I respected their read of the families *and* heard exactly where the plan broke in execution.

Boundaries still show up. I will absolutely say, "that's incomplete" when someone is applying stage-one optimism to a stage-three launch. The difference is I say it after I've grounded the room in why their work matters and what the stakes are for the family sitting across from us. That combination is why they let me keep the mic even when I'm telling them a plan won't work.

Most leaders either stay soft and get ignored or go sharp first and burn trust. Warm then sharp gives you both. Start with empathy so people stay in the conversation, then make the decision unavoidable. That's how you lead rooms that actually execute, not just nod along.
