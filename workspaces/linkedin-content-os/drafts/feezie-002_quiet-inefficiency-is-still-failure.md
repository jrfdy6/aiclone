## First-pass draft

The most dangerous systems are not the chaotic ones. They are the ones that limp along just well enough for everyone to normalize the friction.

At Fusion, most of the reporting surfaces technically existed. Salesforce, Power BI, SharePoint—there was always a link to click. But duplicated records, stale filters, and three different "final" spreadsheets meant no one trusted the view. People spent hours stitching screenshots for leadership or refreshing tabs hoping the data would settle. Quiet inefficiency felt like “just how it is.”

When we rebuilt the Salesforce dashboard, the goal was not prettier charts. We designed one page that told recruiters who to call, showed leadership which territories were stalling, and gave referrals proof that we were actually moving. Meetings jumped by more than 20% and referrals improved by more than 50% once the dashboard made the next move obvious. The system did not change because we yelled louder. It changed because the friction finally disappeared.

So if your org is “fine” but everyone is working twice as hard for the same output, that is failure. Normalized drag is still a red flag.
