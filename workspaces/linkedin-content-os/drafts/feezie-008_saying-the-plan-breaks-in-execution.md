## First-pass draft

Most rooms don’t need more optimism. They need someone to say, “this breaks on Tuesday.” I learned that the hard way. I used to let leadership pitch flawless decks while I politely waited for my turn. Execution always suffered because no one wanted to be the first person to say the emperor had no workflows.

Last quarter a regional team wanted to “launch” a new referral sprint in three states at once. The plan sounded incredible—daily coffees, referral contests, automated follow-up. But the staffing chart showed two coordinators covering six campuses and no coverage for neurodivergent families who needed longer intake. Instead of nodding, I started with the part I liked (“your targeting logic is right”) and then drew the calendar to show where it snapped. Day four required 37 outbound calls per person with zero prep time. Day seven overlapped with existing enrollment days. The room went silent, but suddenly we were solving a real problem instead of pitching fantasy.

That is the job. Warm, then sharp. Recognize the intent, then state exactly where execution dies. Once we did, we cut the sprint to one region, layered Coffee-and-Convo style working sessions, and paired every metric with a specific owner. People actually shipped it because the plan finally respected reality.

Leadership is not telling people “great job” while secretly fixing it later. Leadership is giving everyone permission to stop performing and start operating.
