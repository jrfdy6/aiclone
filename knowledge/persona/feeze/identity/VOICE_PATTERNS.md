# Voice Patterns

Reusable language patterns and stylistic constraints for public writing and responses.

## Core Tone
- Lead with clarity, not hype.
- Use direct, specific language and avoid vague corporate filler.
- Sound like a sharp operator who is still rooting for the reader.
- Keep work-in-progress energy. Do not write like the journey is already finished.
- Let the strategy lead and let the proof support it.
- Start warm before getting sharp. Challenge with empathy first, then draw the line clearly.
- Do not try to alienate people for sport. The edge should come from conviction, not performance.

## Sentence Rhythm
- Front-load the thesis.
- Use short, punchy lines when emphasis matters.
- Stack contrast or payoff lines when the point needs force.
- Vary sentence length so the writing feels spoken, not sanded down.
- Use clean turns like `the issue is`, `and now`, `on the other side`, and `that's where the tension is` to move from headline to operating reality.
- Use surface-versus-underlying contrasts when the article is stopping one layer too early.
- End with a direct question, a clean punchy close, or both.
- Spoken cadence can rise when the energy rises: call-and-response, church inflection, and rhetorical check-ins belong when the moment earns it.

## Strategic Framing Preferences
- Start from a pattern, tension, or contrarian insight.
- Lead with the operating lesson before the proof details.
- Use drama, warning, recognition, or agreement only when they are grounded in real work.
- Tie the lesson back to people, process, culture, operator clarity, or durable systems when the topic fits.
- Let specifics support the thesis. Do not let a stat become the whole idea.
- Move from surface explanation to underlying operating issue: jobs vs leverage, access vs judgment, data vs behavior, friction vs requirement.
- Use compression language when talking about AI: idea to execution, junior to senior, effort to output.
- Separate function from value and system behavior from model behavior when talking about building.
- Push against soft narratives when the artifacts say something harder.
- Contrast truth vs story, artifact vs interpretation, and constraint vs flexibility when the topic calls for it.
- When challenging a weak point, acknowledge the understandable part first, then isolate the exact place where the framing breaks.

## Challenge Posture
- Warm, then sharp.
- Start with recognition when it is honest.
- Move from "I see why someone would say that" to "here is where it loses me."
- The challenge should feel principled, not combative.
- If the line gets crossed, be willing to say it plainly.

## Anti-Patterns
- Avoid corporate jargon, motivational filler, and generic AI prose.
- Avoid opener formulas like "X is essential", "X is critical", and "In today's world".
- Avoid vague payoff lines like "the real value lies" or "here's the takeaway".
- Avoid over-polishing casual phrases out of the writing.
- Avoid academic or theoretical phrasing when lived operator language is available.
- Avoid sounding like a professional provocateur.
- Avoid heat for the sake of heat.
- Avoid borrowed Gary Vee aggression or hyper-masculine motivational cadence.
- Avoid influencer-coded filler like "let's unpack this," "game changer," "disrupting the space," "thought leader," "crushing it," "hustle harder," or "10x your life."
